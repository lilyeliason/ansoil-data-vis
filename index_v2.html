Predicting Antarctic Soil Geochemistry

Antarctica is buried under a dense
blanket of ice, spanning 14.2 million
square kilometers, making it
the largest mass of ice on Earth



Less than 0.18% of this
continent is ice-free.
And in that fraction lies almost
all of Antarctica's biodiversity

And in those exposed regions and buried deep beneath the ice, lies something most people
never think about.

The soil

These arid, gravelly soils are shaped by
extreme cold and dry circumstances.

These conditions have resulted in
unique geochemistry that serves as the
baseline for everything that can survive here.

Despite low biological activity relative to other ecosystems, these soils support a diverse and unique set of organisms.

Without these unique conditions and soils as a baseline, none of this life could survive

Thousands of expeditions have been made over a century
of Antarctic research

And yet the geochemistry of these soils remains largely unmapped

These soils are barometers of environmental change.
If we can't understand them, we can't track what's changing,
or what's being lost.

What Soil Chemistry Reveals

Soil is a chemical record of an ecosystem, climate, and geology of the area.

It's made up of dozens of unique chemical properties that give us specific
insights into different aspects of Antarctic conditions

pH & Electrical Conductivity Water-Soluble Ions Nutrient Availability Heavy & Trace Metals Isotopic Signatures Total Carbon & Nitrogen

And Antarctica is changing.

As the climate warms, the ice is retreating and exposing soils that have been frozen for
thousands of years.

These soils hold valuable records of carbon,
microbial life, and ecosystem history.

Without geochemical baselines, we have no way
to track what is changing.

Each layer reveals a different environmental driver shaped by millions of years of Antarctic isolation.

δ¹⁵N — Nitrogen Isotopes

Why does it look like this?

Coastal hotspots trace penguin rookeries and seal colonies whose waste enriches marine-derived nitrogen. Inland deserts, devoid of animals, show none of this signature.

Phosphorus (P)

Why does it look like this?

Phosphorus concentrations track both bedrock weathering and biological inputs. Coastal zones with high animal activity show elevated values from guano and organic matter decomposition.

Soluble Sodium (Na)

Why does it look like this?

A fading ring around the continent's edges maps the marine aerosol footprint. Coastal winds deposit sea salt directly onto soil; the Transantarctic Mountains block this inland.

Nickel (Ni)

Why does it look like this?

Nickel distribution closely follows mafic and ultramafic rock formations. Its sharp boundaries are a near-perfect trace of igneous intrusions across ice-free zones.

Titanium (Ti)

Why does it look like this?

Stark jagged boundaries trace the Ferrar Dolerite volcanic formations almost perfectly. Chemical weathering is so slow here that bedrock lithology dominates the signal entirely.

Climate Science

Tracking a changing continent

As ice retreats and new soils emerge, we have no baseline for what they contain. Geochemical predictions give us that baseline — so we can measure what is being lost or created as the planet warms.

Field Science

Smarter expeditions

Continent-wide prediction maps tell future researchers exactly where the most scientifically valuable sites are — saving time, money, and the enormous logistical costs of Antarctic fieldwork.

Astrobiology

A window to other worlds

Antarctic ice-free soils are our closest analogue on Earth to the surface of Mars. Understanding what survives here — and why — is a guide to where we might search for life beyond our planet.
