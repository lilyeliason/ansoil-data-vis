The 0.18%
Predicting Antarctic Soil Geochemistry

Antarctica is buried under a dense blanket of ice, spanning 14.2 million square kilometers,
making it the largest mass of ice on Earth.

But less than 0.18% of this continent
is ice-free. And in that fraction lies almost
all of Antarctica's biodiversity.

And the conditions in these regions
are rapidly changing

With the rise of global temperatures,
soils that have been buried under ice
for thousands of years are being exposed
for the first time.

The soils found in these regions are the foundational building blocks of ice-free ecosystems

As climate conditions continue to change, understanding the makeup of these soils becomes essential to
Antarctica's future

So what do we know about them?

Ideal coverage

Ideally, we would have thousands of samples from all across the continent.

This would mean sampling every valley, coastline, and exposed ridge in an extremely remote and inhospitable environment.

Actual sampling
171 samples · 4 regions

Our lab has just 171 samples from 4 regions across
the continent.

This is a mere fraction of what would be needed for a complete analysis.

Outside of our samples, the rest remains largely unknown.

The known

On our 171 samples, we have performed extensive geochemical analysis to come up with 58 unique geochemical characteristics about each data point.

Satellite observation

However, we can also use satellites data to observe the physical properties of
every square meter of Antarctica's surface, whether anyone has sampled there or not.

Lithology Distance to Coast Elevation Temperature Precipitation Slope Aspect Latitude Longitude
The transfer

We can use these shared satellite properties to teach machine learning models to predict geochemical properties at locations that have never been sampled.

The result

So then from just 171 points, we gain a more complete window into the geochemical makeup of soils across the entire continent.

So even if we can make predictions,
why does it matter?

Climate science

Tracking a changing continent.

As ice retreats and new soils emerge, we have no baseline for what they contain. Geochemical predictions give us that baseline so we can measure what is being lost or created as the planet warms.

Field science

Smarter expeditions.

Continent-wide prediction maps can tell future researchers exactly where to go, saving time, money, and resources.

Antarctic dry valley

Mars surface — Curiosity rover

Astrobiology

A window to other worlds.

Antarctic ice-free soils are our closest analogue on Earth to the surface of Mars. Understanding what survives here — and why — is a guide to where we might look for life beyond our planet.
